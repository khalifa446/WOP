What is Psychology?

Scientific study of behavior & mental processes

Psychology uses empirical research and observation to understand thoughts, emotions, and actions.

Internal mental processes

Explores memory, perception, language, learning, problem-solving, and decision making.

Human experience & self-growth

Focuses on personal development, self-actualization and potential for fulfillment.

Applied psychology

Uses knowledge to improve mental health, well-being, education, and social issues.

Wide scope

Encompasses clinical, social, cognitive, biological, developmental, forensic, and more.

Origins and Evolution of Psychology

Psychology’s roots trace back to ancient civilizations, where questions about the soul, consciousness, and behavior were raised by philosophers in Egypt, Greece, India, China, and Persia. Greek philosophers like Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle pondered the nature of mind and human behavior. In India, Buddhist psychology introduced ideas about perception and consciousness. Islamic scholars such as Al-Farabi and Avicenna wrote extensively about mental health and emotional functioning, centuries before psychology emerged in the West.

The term "psychology" comes from the Greek words "psyche" (soul) and "logos" (study). However, psychology did not become a distinct scientific discipline until Wilhelm Wundt established the first experimental psychology lab in 1879 in Leipzig, Germany. Wundt aimed to analyze the structure of the mind using introspection, laying the foundation for structuralism. As psychology progressed, it evolved through various schools of thought, each expanding its scope and methodology.

History of Psychology

  1. Ancient Roots: Early philosophical interest in mind, emotion, and behavior (Egypt, Greece, China, India).
  2. 1500 BCE: Ebers Papyrus references mental disorders.
  3. 11th century – Avicenna: Linked emotions and physical health.
  4. 1758 – William Battie: Wrote "Treatise on Madness".
  5. 1879 – Wilhelm Wundt: Established the first psychology lab in Leipzig.
  6. Early 1900s: Freud’s psychoanalysis, behaviorism by Watson and Skinner.
  7. 1950s–60s: Humanistic and cognitive revolutions (Maslow, Rogers, Chomsky).
  8. Today: Focus on neuroscience, AI, biopsychology, and global mental health.

Major Schools of Thought

Structuralism

Wundt & Titchener: studied structure of consciousness using introspection.

Functionalism

James & Dewey: focused on the purpose of consciousness and behavior.

Behaviorism

Watson & Skinner: emphasized observable behaviors and conditioning.

Psychoanalysis

Freud: emphasized unconscious motives and early childhood experiences.

Gestalt Psychology

Perception of the whole rather than parts—how we organize experiences.

Humanistic Psychology

Maslow & Rogers: focused on personal growth, empathy, and self-actualization.

Cognitive Psychology

Studied mental processes like memory, attention, and problem-solving.

Biopsychology

Explores how brain and biology affect behavior and mental processes.

The 14 Branches of Psychology

As the field matured, psychology was divided into 14 major branches to specialize research and application. This division allows psychologists to focus on specific dimensions of mental processes and behavior. These branches emerged over time in response to societal needs, scientific advancement, and philosophical exploration.

Why this division? As psychology grew during the 20th century, no single method or theory could explain the full range of human thoughts and behaviors. Thus, it became essential to organize the discipline into focused subfields — each with its own methods, applications, and research goals. This allowed psychology to be applied in medicine, education, industry, law, technology, and beyond.

When did this happen? The division into branches began primarily in the early to mid-20th century. As the demand for psychological expertise expanded — especially after the World Wars — universities, research institutions, and governments began recognizing the need for specialized psychological services in different domains.

1. Clinical Psychology

Diagnoses and treats mental, emotional, and behavioral disorders.

2. Counseling Psychology

Focuses on personal and interpersonal functioning across the lifespan.

3. Developmental Psychology

Studies psychological growth and change throughout life.

4. Educational Psychology

Examines how people learn and retain knowledge.

5. Forensic Psychology

Applies psychology to legal and criminal investigations.

6. Health Psychology

Focuses on how biology, psychology, and social factors affect health.

7. Industrial-Organizational Psychology

Studies human behavior in workplaces.

8. Neuropsychology

Explores relationships between brain systems and behavior.

9. Social Psychology

Examines how people influence and are influenced by others.

10. Experimental Psychology

Uses scientific methods to study behavior and cognition.

11. Comparative Psychology

Studies behavior across different species.

12. Sports Psychology

Focuses on psychological aspects of performance and exercise.

13. Environmental Psychology

Explores how environments affect behavior and well-being.

14. Positive Psychology

Focuses on strengths, virtues, and factors that contribute to happiness.
